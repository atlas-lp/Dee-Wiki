# Digital Transformation: 4-Module Course Outline (68-Minute Architecture)

A unified course architecture synthesising Venkatraman's Digital Matrix framework, Gupta's all-in digital strategy thesis, Rogers' CC-DIV model, Westerman's four-phase compass, and Mani's ISB modular programme design. The course progresses from strategic imperative to practical execution across four modules, each calibrated to **17 minutes of reading time** (total: 68 minutes).

| Module | Title | Theme | Reading Time |
|--------|-------|--------|--------------|

## Module 1 — The Digital Imperative (17 minutes)

**Central Question:** Why can't incumbents afford to treat digital as a peripheral initiative?

**Core Arguments:**
- Digitisation is not a one-time project but an ongoing evolution reshaping how problems are framed and solved across economies, industries, and society (Venkatraman).
- Leaders who achieve transformative results integrate digital strategy into overall business strategy rather than running it as a separate workstream (Gupta).
- Creating an independent digital unit — the "speedboat" model — typically fails because the speedboat takes off but does little to turn the large ship (Gupta).
- Relying solely on technology to reduce costs implicitly assumes that technology will not fundamentally change the business — a dangerous assumption (Gupta).

**Key Concepts Introduced:**
- Digital transformation as strategic imperative vs. operational upgrade
- The failure modes of siloed digital initiatives
- The all-in vs. piecemeal choice

**Suggested Reading Anchors:**
- Gupta, *Driving Digital Strategy* — Introduction and Chapter 1
- Venkatraman, *The Digital Matrix* — Prologue: The New Business Game

## Module 2 — Reimagining the Business (17 minutes)

**Central Question:** What does it mean to fundamentally redefine what your business does and how it creates value?

**Core Arguments:**
- Business scope redefinition requires leaders to ask not "how do we digitise our existing model" but "what new problems can we solve for customers with digital capabilities."
- Business model innovation in the digital era moves from product-centric to platform and ecosystem logic — companies co-create offerings by working in dynamic business networks rather than acting in isolation (Venkatraman).
- Corporations should be understood as portfolios of capabilities assembled through networks of relationships; successful firms know when to connect to emerging ecosystems and when to pull back (Venkatraman).
- Gupta's three-pillar structure — reimagining the business, reevaluating the value chain, reconnecting with customers — provides an actionable decomposition of transformation scope.

**Key Concepts Introduced:**
- Business scope redefinition
- Platform and ecosystem logic
- Value chain reevaluation
- Omnichannel and customer reconnection

**Suggested Reading Anchors:**
- Gupta, *Driving Digital Strategy* — Parts I and II (Business Reimagination, Value Chain)
- Venkatraman, *The Digital Matrix* — Chapter on Ecosystems and Capability Portfolios

## Module 3 — Navigating the Digital Matrix (17 minutes)

**Central Question:** Who are the players reshaping your industry, and what phase of disruption are you in?

**Core Arguments:**
- The Digital Matrix is a two-axis framework: players (Digital Giants, Industry Incumbents, Tech Entrepreneurs) × phases (Experimentation at the Edge, Collision at the Core, Reinvention at the Root) (Venkatraman).
- Each phase demands a different strategic posture — experimentation tolerance in Phase 1, competitive sharpness in Phase 2, and identity-level reinvention in Phase 3.
- Digital Giants (e.g., Amazon, Google, Alibaba) are not just tech companies — they are capability platforms that can enter any industry where data and algorithms create value.
- Incumbents must understand competitive dynamics as neither purely competitive nor purely cooperative — dynamic coalitions and co-opetition are the norm.
- The Digital Matrix Command Center is a conceptual tool for tracking a company's moves and competitors' actions through all three phases.

## Module 4 — Executing the All-In Strategy (17 minutes)

**Central Question:** How do you organise, lead, and make the winning moves that turn strategy into transformation?

**Core Arguments:**
- Three Winning Moves anchor execution: (1) orchestrating ecosystems, (2) collaborating to co-create new capabilities, and (3) amplifying human talent with machines (Venkatraman).
- Organisational architecture must be redesigned — not just restructured — to support digital business models; resistance from existing structures (e.g., country heads unconvinced by digital innovations) is a primary execution barrier (Gupta).
- Measurement and analytics must evolve from lagging financial indicators to leading digital metrics that track customer engagement, ecosystem health, and capability development.
- Mani's ISB modular programme design principle reinforces that transformation learning itself should be modular, progressive, and application-focused — mirroring the course architecture here.
- Leadership mindset shift: from managing assets to orchestrating ecosystems; from competitive moats to dynamic alliances.

**Key Concepts Introduced:**

- Ecosystem orchestration
- Human–machine collaboration
- Digital organisational architecture
- Transformation measurement and analytics

**Suggested Reading Anchors:**
- Venkatraman, *The Digital Matrix* — Chapters on Winning Moves and Organisational Architecture
- Gupta, *Driving Digital Strategy* — Part III (Reconnecting with Customers) and Conclusion

## Design Principles

1. **Progressive logic**: Each module builds on the prior — imperative → model → landscape → execution.
2. **Framework plurality**: No single framework dominates; Venkatraman, Gupta, Rogers, Westerman, and Mani are integrated, not layered.
3. **Consistent depth**: 17-minute calibration ensures parity across modules; instructors should trim or expand case examples to maintain pacing.
4. **Application anchors**: Each module closes with a strategic question that learners can apply to their own organisation.

---

## Strategic Questions per Module (Discussion / Assessment Use)

- **M1**: Where in your organisation is digital still treated as a separate initiative rather than core strategy — and what is the cost of that separation?
- **M2**: If you were to reimagine your business as a platform rather than a product company, what capabilities would you need to build or borrow?
- **M3**: Which phase of the Digital Matrix is your industry in? Who are the Digital Giants, Incumbents, and Entrepreneurs reshaping it?
- **M4**: Which of the Three Winning Moves is your organisation best positioned to execute first — and what organisational barriers stand in the way?
